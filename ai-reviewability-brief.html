AI reviewability brief

Design reviewable interfaces for AI-assisted content

A compact editorial and product brief for teams that need AI-assisted drafts to remain inspectable before publication. It separates source facts, generated claims, missing evidence, privacy boundaries, and final reviewer decisions.

Problem

AI drafts often look finished before they are reviewable

A polished paragraph can hide unsupported claims, inferred steps, stale source material, private details, and unresolved publication blockers. Reviewability treats those risks as interface requirements rather than editorial cleanup.

Design model

Separate the draft from the decision trail

LayerInterface responsibility
Source factsName the source material and keep it visible near the generated claim.
Generated claimsShow which statements were drafted by an AI-assisted process and still need review.
Missing evidenceSurface blockers instead of allowing a smooth draft to pass quietly.
Privacy boundaryKeep credentials, account pages, private tickets, payment details, and customer exports out of the publication flow.
Reviewer decisionRecord whether the content is approved, revised, held, or blocked, with an owner.

Article outline

Fresh long-form writing shape

  1. Why AI-generated prose can mislead reviewers by looking complete too early.
  2. How to treat reviewability as a UX and product requirement.
  3. How to distinguish confirmed facts from generated or inferred claims.
  4. How to design reviewer paths for confirm, revise, block, and publish decisions.
  5. Practical patterns: source-linked claim cards, blocker banners, privacy checks, and publish-readiness summaries.
  6. A small knowledge-base update example with visible evidence and publication boundaries.
  7. Anti-patterns: one-click publish flows, unexplained confidence scores, hidden evidence, and vague disclaimers.
